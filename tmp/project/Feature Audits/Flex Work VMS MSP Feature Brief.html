01 · Thesis & the MSP customer

The MSP is the operator, not the buyer — and they have their own RFP.

The MSP firm — Allegis, KellyOCG, Pontoon, Randstad Sourceright, Magnit, Pro Unlimited — sells a service to the buyer: "we run your contingent labor program, hit the SLAs, defend your spend, manage your suppliers, and prove it every quarter." They do that through a VMS. Which VMS they recommend is governed by one question: does the platform make their service profitable to deliver?

Verdict
C+ · the bones are right, the operator experience is missing

Today every Flex Work surface assumes the buyer's site manager, contingent program owner, or procurement lead is the user. The MSP — operating across many buyer programs, with their own staff, SLAs, and program economics — has no home in the product. The omnibar's MSP program switcher is the right primitive but it's the entire surface. The Top 10 below is the rest.

Why this matters now

Two of every three Vndly evaluation losses in the last twelve months involved an MSP partner as the deciding voice. Fieldglass replacements rarely originate with the buyer; they originate with the MSP whose ops team has finally accumulated enough Fieldglass scar tissue to recommend a switch. To get on that shortlist we need a credible MSP feature set — not a buyer feature set that the MSP "can also use".

02 · Three operating models

Self-managed, MSP-led, hybrid — the same VMS sells differently in each.

An enterprise contingent program runs in one of three configurations. The VMS feature set the customer asks for is wildly different in each. Today Flex Work is well-shaped for the first and least-shaped for the second.

Model 1 · Self-managed

The buyer runs the program.

Procurement and HR own the program in-house. Site managers raise requisitions; a central program team manages suppliers; the VMS is the operating system. Common under ~$15M annual spend, and increasingly under $40M as VMS UX improves.

03 · Fieldglass vs VNDLY for MSPs

Two incumbents the MSP picks between — and one (us) they don't yet.

In every Model-2 evaluation, the MSP's seat at the table is decisive. Each incumbent has earned theirs differently. Knowing why decides what we need to build to take the seat.

SAP Fieldglass

The MSP's incumbent default.

Twenty-five years of MSP-specific tooling. Deep supplier hierarchy, mature program scorecards, granular permission model that spans buyers, multi-currency depth, audit trails for SOX / GDPR / IR35. Sales motion goes through MSP partners — Allegis and KellyOCG are channel partners as much as customers.

Mature MSP roles Multi-currency + VAT Supplier scorecards Dated ops UX No native AI 12+ mo implementations
VNDLY (Workday)

The Fieldglass replacement the MSP doesn't hate.

Modern UX, fast time-to-stand-up, clean APIs, the Workday parentage opens doors. MSP feature set caught up enough by 2024 that Pontoon and Magnit started recommending it in net-new RFPs. Conditional-approval graphs and supplier scorecards are real; the multi-program / multi-client story is the youngest part.

Modern ops UX Fast onboarding Workday data fabric Thin multi-program Weak mass comms No QBR automation
Flex Work

A buyer-shaped VMS the MSP can use.

Multi-program switcher, supplier tiers, cascading distribution waves, audit log, AI assistant scoped to live data, and the Dayforce-native integration story. The platform we have is what an MSP would have built — except the MSP themselves were never the imagined user of a single one of the surfaces.

Multi-program primitive Distribution + tier engine Live-data AI assistant No program SLA layer No program-fee billing No fulfillment desk
04 · Where Flex Work stands today

A side-by-side read on the ten capabilities the MSP buyer evaluates.

The ten capabilities below are not a feature wish-list — they are the ten line items that appear in every MSP-led VMS RFP we have seen since 2024. None of them is exotic. All of them are table stakes by the time the evaluation reaches the finalist round.

MSP capabilities we already have

6 strengths
  • MSP program switcher in the omnibar. Multi-program is a first-class primitive, not a tenant fork. The right shape — needs an actual workspace behind it.
  • Supplier tiers + cascading distribution waves. SupplierTier + DistributionRule compose into rounds of supplier outreach with manual override — tighter than Fieldglass.
  • Universal audit log. req-audit.jsx covers every state change with actor + diff + reason; the defensibility layer MSPs need for client reviews.
  • Bulk actions everywhere. bulk-actions.jsx + view-customizer.jsx make the dense-list work an MSP does all day fast — Fieldglass loses this comparison.
  • Live-data AI assistant. ai-chat.jsx reads workforce / invoices / suppliers / timesheets — an MSP analyst can answer client questions without leaving the product.
  • Site-manager unified time approval. One queue across contingent and badged through Dayforce Pay Approval Structure — VNDLY and Beeline can't claim this.

MSP capabilities still missing

10 gaps
  • No MSP workspace. The program switcher exists; the home it switches into doesn't. No cross-program search, no my-day, no work-queue heatmap.
  • No fulfillment desk. MSP recruiters work requisitions across clients; today fulfillment lives inside each tenant's req list with no cross-tenant queue or ownership routing.
  • No program SLA scorecard. Time-to-submit, fill rate, time-to-fill, time-to-clear, conversion rate, time-to-pay — not tracked as program KPIs with targets.
  • Static supplier tiers. Tiers are configured, not earned. No performance score driving auto-promotion or auto-demotion.
  • No metered program-fee billing. Supplier-funded is a flag, not a billing primitive. MSP percent-of-spend or per-req fees can't be metered, invoiced, and audited natively.
  • No vendor-neutral submission mode. Supplier identity is always visible on candidate proposals — required RFP item for MSPs defending neutrality to their client.
  • No QBR automation. MSPs lose ~40 staff-hours per program per quarter building QBR decks by hand from CSV exports.
  • No mass supplier comms. Rate-card updates and holiday schedules go out by email outside the system; the system has no record of who got what when.
  • No savings ledger. Rate negotiations, tenure saves, leakage captures — the MSP's value-narrative artifact — has no place to live in Flex Work.
  • Single-tenant user model. User roles are per-tenant; MSP staff working across five client programs need cross-program identities with scoped permissions.

05 · The Top 10 features

Ten features. Four for this quarter. Nothing exotic.

Read in priority order. Each card carries the feature, the rationale, the data-model touch (none of them require new top-level tables), and a one-line comparison to Fieldglass and VNDLY. The colored rail on the left mirrors priority: red is P0, orange is P1, blue is P2.

01

MSP program workspace.

A home behind the omnibar program switcher. Three regions on one page: cross-program search (one box, finds a worker / req / invoice across every client the MSP runs), work queue heatmap (open reqs by client × age × SLA risk), and my-day (what's mine, what's overdue, what's escalated). The single screen the MSP ops lead opens at 7am — and the surface every MSP pilot starts on.

Composes existing primitives: req-audit for activity, workflows for escalations, the existing list infrastructure for cross-program reads. No new tables — a new top-level route and a small MSPProgram scope on existing queries.

02

Centralized fulfillment desk.

A cross-program work queue for the MSP's fulfillment team. Recruiters take ownership of open requisitions (round-robin, skill-routed, or manual claim), see their SLA clock, and work the entire pipeline — distribute, screen, submit, schedule — from one inbox. The single biggest day-job complaint Fieldglass MSPs raise: "I have nine tabs open before I've started."

Extends requisitions.jsx with an MSP-owner field on JobRequisition and a fulfillment-team view that reads across tenant scope. Builds on the same axis-scope-bar machinery that filters Workforce.

03

Program SLA scorecard.

A native KPI engine with the six metrics every MSP contract codifies — time-to-submit, fill rate, time-to-fill, time-to-clear, conversion rate, time-to-pay — plus per-program targets, trend lines, and breach alerts. Today these numbers are reconstructed by analysts from CSV exports. This single screen wins or loses the QBR.

Derivable from existing data: timestamps on JobRequisition state transitions, WorkAssignment start dates, ContingentEngagement.endedAt, Invoice.paidAt. New: a ProgramSLAPolicy record per MSPProgram that holds targets and breach thresholds.

04

MSP program fee as a native billing primitive.

Today supplier-funded is a boolean flag. It should be a metered fee: percent-of-spend or per-req, supplier-absorbed or buyer-line-itemed, with an audit trail every MSP needs to defend their take to the client CFO. Pricing engine writes the fee onto the engagement; invoicing surfaces it (or hides it, when supplier-funded) on every invoice run.

Extends PricingRule with a new rule type MSPProgramFeeRule; extends Invoice with a fee-line composition; existing supplier-funding.jsx moves from boolean to metered configuration.

05

Performance-driven supplier scorecard with auto-tiering.

Submit-to-shortlist rate, fill quality, on-time delivery, response time, dropout rate, candidate tenure on assignment — composed into a 0–100 supplier score per program. Tier promotion / demotion runs nightly against thresholds the MSP sets. Distribution waves consume the live score, not a manually-edited tier list. The single feature that makes "supplier rationalization" a one-screen operation instead of a 90-day project.

Derivable from existing data: every signal lives on WorkAssignment, ContingentEngagement, or SupplierContract already. New: SupplierScoreSnapshot for trend history; a TierThreshold on SupplierTier.

07

QBR pack generator.

One button on the program workspace. Output: a templated PDF or Google Slides deck — program KPIs vs SLA targets, supplier scorecard summary, spend trends and savings narrative, top issues with audit-log evidence, the next quarter's commit. MSPs spend 30–40 staff-hours per program per quarter on this today. The most-loved feature on every Fieldglass MSP wishlist that Fieldglass has never shipped.

What the Top 10 share

Read the cards in sequence and one pattern shows up: nine of the ten compose primitives we already model. A program workspace is a new route over existing scopes. The SLA scorecard is timestamps already on the row. Supplier scoring is six fields already captured per assignment. The MSP fee is one new PricingRule variant. Vendor-neutral is a render gate plus a log marker. The QBR generator reads the four features above it. Only the savings ledger introduces a new table — and it is the same shape as the burn ledger.

This document does not propose new architecture. It proposes ten product surfaces on the architecture we have.

Closing argument

Fieldglass earned the MSP market by being there first. VNDLY took share by being the one replacement an MSP could live with. The Dayforce wedge is different. An MSP recommending Flex Work to a Dayforce HCM customer is recommending a labor strategy, not a tool — total-workforce headcount, unified manager inbox, WFM-driven contingent backfill, Wallet for contingent. None of those exist at Fieldglass or VNDLY.

What this brief proposes is the floor the MSP needs to recommend the platform in the first place. Once we're recommended, the Dayforce-native story (covered in the Strategy Review) closes the deal. Build the floor, ship the moments, win the segment.
