Companion to the v1.4 Architecture doc. This document does not propose UI changes — only a directional read of capability gaps and the order to close them in.

Flex Work VMS · Strategy Review · v1.0

Three customers, three verdicts.

The platform's three-rung strategy is right. The architecture is ready for it. The product reality is uneven: frontline is competitive but still missing the table-stakes worker-engagement layer JoinedUp built its category on. The broader VMS surface is wide but shallow — the shapes exist, the depth does not. And the Dayforce integration story is brilliant on paper but barely visible on screen: we adopted the data models, we have not yet shipped the moments where Dayforce + Flex Work is undeniably better than either alone.

01 · Thesis & three verdicts

Win frontline now. Earn Dayforce-native. Take the broader market.

Each rung is a real fight against a real incumbent. We grade ourselves separately on each, because the same product is a different competitor in each room.

Rung 1 · Frontline VMS

Beat JoinedUp by Beeline.

High-volume agency shift work — manufacturing, hospitality, retail, healthcare, logistics. Bread-and-butter Flex Work today. JoinedUp set the modern bar with worker-mobile-first ops; we have the data model, not the experience.

Verdict. B+ on data + admin tooling. C on the worker-facing comms loop that decides whether a 5am shift actually gets filled.
Rung 2 · Overall VMS

Take Vndly's market.

Generalist enterprise VMS: Assignment + Project + SOW + IC + EOR. Helios is the seeded tenant with every flag on — and it shows the shapes are present but the depth that converts Vndly evaluations isn't there yet.

Verdict. B− on breadth. C− on depth — SOW change orders, IC compliance engine, rate intelligence, candidate matching.
Rung 3 · Dayforce integration

Make Dayforce the only credible total-labor pitch.

Embed inside Dayforce Core, adopt every shared data model, ship the demoable cross-product moments that no standalone VMS can clone. The architecture is done. The visible product moments aren't.

Verdict. A− on contract design. C on lived experience — the customer can't see the integration in the product yet.
The bet

The three rungs are not parallel investments. Rung 1 funds Rung 2 (frontline references buy us the right to talk Professional / SOW). Rung 2 funds Rung 3 (a complete VMS is a more attractive consolidation target). Rung 3 defends Rungs 1 and 2 (no standalone VMS can clone the Dayforce-native pitch). Cut Rung 1, the others starve. Skip Rung 3, we're a feature company competing with Vndly on features — which we lose for the next 24 months.

02 · Frontline VMS

Beat JoinedUp where JoinedUp lives — on the worker's phone.

JoinedUp by Beeline wins frontline because it treats the worker as the primary user of the system, not the buyer. SMS-first comms, sub-60s shift offer-to-accept loops, predictive fill scoring, geofenced clock — these aren't fancy features, they're the table stakes a frontline ops director will ask about in the first 15 minutes of every demo. Fieldglass is a non-starter here (built for procurement, not ops). Vndly is roughly where we are: solid data model, light worker experience.

Verdict
B+ · in the room with JoinedUp; not winning it

The buyer-side surfaces — requisition intake, supplier distribution waves, timesheet approval, supplier tiers, late-cancel fee tiers — are genuinely competitive. What's missing is the worker-side conversion engine: broadcast → response → confirm → arrive → clock, run inside 10 minutes for a 5am shift. We have worker-mobile.jsx, talent-pools.jsx, credentialing.jsx, remove-worker-panel.jsx with SLA tiers, schedule-console.jsx, roster builder. We do not have the SMS broadcast loop, the no-show prediction, the geofenced clock, or the worker self-scheduling marketplace.

What we already do well

8 strengths
  • Multi-supplier distribution with cascading rounds. DistributionRule + SupplierTier + manual overrides. Tighter than Fieldglass; on par with JoinedUp.
  • Site-manager-first timesheet approval. One queue across contingent and badged via Dayforce Pay Approval Structure — JoinedUp can't claim this.
  • Late-cancel SLA tiering on worker removal. RW_SLA_TIERS in remove-worker-panel.jsx auto-resolves fee based on hours-to-shift — Vndly and JoinedUp both still do this in spreadsheets.
  • Talent pools as first-class. talent-pools.jsx + talent-pool-rules.jsx separate eligibility from execution — the right shape.
  • Credentialing tied to Dayforce Qualifications. Single source of truth, not a parallel state machine.
  • Bulk-action surfaces on every list. bulk-actions.jsx + view-customizer.jsx — the dense-data UX is genuinely good.
  • MSP-program switcher in the omnibar — the right primitive for enterprise procurement teams running multiple programs.
  • Schedule console with multi-day shift view — denser and faster than JoinedUp's current calendar.

Where JoinedUp pulls ahead

9 gaps
  • SMS-first worker comms. JoinedUp blasts open shifts via SMS to ranked pool members and accepts replies as confirmations. We have no broadcast channel and no SMS provider integration.
  • Worker self-scheduling marketplace. Workers see open shifts in their app and self-claim. Today our worker mobile is view-only on the schedule.
  • Geofenced + biometric clock-in. Required RFP item for every frontline procurement deal > 1,000 workers. We currently have no geofence enforcement.
  • No-show prediction. JoinedUp ranks workers by reliability + late-cancel history and surfaces "fill confidence: 62%" 24h before shift. We track none of these signals.
  • Direct-source-first fills. Open shifts go to internal pool / past workers before distribution to suppliers — saves 8–15% markup. We always start with the supplier tier.
  • Re-engagement of past workers. "Workers who clocked at this site >3× in last 90d" is one-click for JoinedUp; we have no surface for it.
  • Pattern-based bulk requisitions. "10 workers × 12 shifts/week × 4 weeks at Plant #02" as a single request. We have requisition templates but not pattern intake.
  • In-shift live board. Walk-on capture, no-show flagging, real-time confirmed-vs-clocked count. Our schedule console is a pre-shift tool, not a during-shift one.
  • Incentive overlays on hard-to-fill shifts. JoinedUp lets ops add a $5/hr surge for a thin shift; we have no surge primitive in Pricing.

The frontline closing argument

Three of the four highest-leverage frontline gaps are user-experience layers on top of data we already have. Reliability score → derive from TimePair history. Pattern intake → templates + a loop. Direct-source-first → reorder existing cascade waves. The fourth (geofenced clock) is a worker-mobile extension with one new field on the punch payload.

None of this is platform work. It's product work. The architecture's already paid for.

03 · Overall VMS

Helios with every flag on — the breadth is right, the depth is not.

Helios Power Generation is the seeded tenant designed to prove Rung 3: every engagement type on, every supplier type on, Frontline + Professional job catalogs, supplier-funded. It is the right shape — one Requisitions list with Shift / Assignment / Project / SOW chips, one Workforce list with Agency / IC / EOR axis, one Invoices list spanning hourly and milestone billing. The shape is what wins Rung 3 evals on slide one. Where we lose them is slides three through eight, when a Vndly demo gets specific about SOW change orders, IC compliance, rate intelligence, and AI-ranked candidate proposals.

Verdict
B− · competitive on the org chart; thin on the deals that close

Helios proves the type-blind data model works — universal scopes filter cleanly across surfaces with engagement-type chip-bars and supplier-type axes. The Professional flow exists (professional-work.jsx, professional-req-details.jsx, professional-onboard.jsx); the SOW flow exists (sow.jsx, sow-dashboard.jsx, milestones, change-order panels referenced); IC and EOR onboarding flows exist. But every one of these is a shape that needs to harden into a workflow before it survives an enterprise evaluation against Vndly.

What Helios already proves

7 strengths
  • One Requisitions list, four engagement types. Chip-bar filter, not separate destinations. Vndly still ships SOW as a sibling tab to Worker Requests; we don't.
  • One Workforce list with Agency / IC / EOR axis. Native axis filter via axis-scope-bar.jsx; supplier-type axis derived from supplier-types-config.jsx. Genuinely modern.
  • Per-org engagement-type configuration. Settings → Configuration → Engagement Types. The right place; tenant-scoped; flips activate downstream lists.
  • SOW with milestones + supplier-funding pre-selected. Helios ships supplier-funded; SOWAgreement + MilestoneAcceptance are first-class.
  • Project burn ledger. ProjectBudget + BurnLedgerEntry — the right primitive for T&M-against-budget. Fieldglass still doesn't ship a burn view this clean.
  • Supplier contract rate-card + markup engine. SupplierContract + PricingRule + MarkupRule write to ContingentEngagement.billRate — the right composition.
  • AI assistant scoped to live data. ai-chat.jsx reads from workforce / invoices / requisitions / timesheets / suppliers — answers "what did we spend last week at the Reactor Yard" with real numbers, not a chatbot script.

Where Vndly wins the deal today

10 gaps
  • SOW change-order workflow. Vndly's SOW carries draft → redline → amendment → countersign with full version history. We have the agreement object; we don't have the lifecycle.
  • IC classification engine. Vndly runs a ~25-question worker-classification questionnaire that produces a risk score and audit log. We have an IC supplier type and an onboarding flow; we don't have the compliance defensibility.
  • Co-employment & tenure alerts. Auto-track days-on-assignment per worker; alert before 18-month / 24-month limits; force off-board policy. We don't track tenure as a worker-level state.
  • Rate benchmark intelligence. Vndly markets "market rate by skill × location × tenure" pulled from program data. Our rate cards are configured; we don't surface benchmarks.
  • AI-ranked candidate proposals. Supplier proposes 5 candidates; system ranks them against the requisition's must-haves and historical fill quality. We list proposals; we don't score them.
  • Conditional approval routing. "Over $50K → CFO; over $150K → CFO + CHRO; remote SOW → Legal." Vndly's approval builder is a drag-drop graph. Our workflows.jsx is closer to linear approval chains.
  • Reusable SOW catalog by service category. Pre-priced services with standard deliverables ("WordPress migration · $18K · 4 wks"). Buyers consume from a catalog, not draft from blank.
  • Multi-currency + FX handling on invoices. Invoices have a currency field; cross-currency consolidation, FX-as-of date, and rolled-up GL push aren't there.
  • VAT / global indirect tax. salesTax flag is a US-style overlay. Enterprise European and APAC deals require multi-jurisdiction VAT with reverse-charge logic.
  • Resource forecasting. Vndly's "next-quarter contingent need" forecast pulls from BU plans + historical fills. We don't forecast.

The overall-VMS closing argument

Helios already wins the "can your VMS handle our four engagement types in one product?" question. It loses the "can it handle our four engagement types defensibly?" question — the SOW change-order trail, the IC classification audit log, the tenure-policy enforcement, the conditional approvals. 04 · Dayforce integration

The data models are adopted. The killer moments aren't.

The architecture is excellent: Flex Work runs as its own Rails monolith + Postgres + deploy pipeline, embeds in Dayforce Core via iframe with SSO + theming + postMessage, and adopts the Dayforce data model for every shared primitive — Employee, OrgUnit, Job, JobAssignment, Position, WorkAssignment, TimePair, LaborMetricCode, JobRequisition, UserRole. The eight SOAP integrations in the architecture doc cover the wire. What's missing is the lived product moment — the screens where a customer feels that Flex Work + Dayforce is a single labor strategy, not two systems shaking hands behind a curtain.

Verdict
B− · brilliant on paper; mostly invisible to the customer today

Adopting the data model is necessary, not sufficient. The Dayforce-native pitch is won or lost on a handful of demoable cross-product moments — a unified inbox, an integrated WFM-to-contingent forecast handoff, a temp-to-perm flow that takes 30 seconds, a total-workforce headcount number that includes contingent. None of these require new platform work. They are product surfaces that compose primitives we already adopt.

The eight cross-product moments — what no standalone VMS can build

01

WFM demand forecast → auto-draft contingent requisitions.

Dayforce Workforce Management forecasts a Friday-shift gap of 24 heads at Plant #02 from historical demand. Flex Work consumes the forecast (via the EmployeeWorkAssignment + scheduling integration), pre-drafts a Shift requisition with role / qty / time / site, and surfaces it in the manager's inbox for one-tap publish. Vndly cannot ship this — they don't own WFM.

Needs: read access to WFM forecasts (new Dayforce-side report XRefCode); a draft state on JobRequisition wired to the WFM event bus.

02

Total workforce headcount — one number, badged + contingent.

Dayforce HR Analytics' headcount tile reads Employee rows including workerType = ContingentWorker. Add a 1-line filter chip on the tile ("Include contingent: On") and finance leadership sees the only honest total-workforce number in the market. Today most enterprises stitch this from spreadsheets.

Needs: Dayforce-side tile filter; nothing on Flex Work — the rows already sync via Integration 02.

03

Unified manager inbox across HR + contingent.

A site manager today has two inboxes: Dayforce approvals (PTO, schedule changes, badged time) and Flex Work approvals (timesheet, supplier confirm, late-cancel waiver). One inbox in Dayforce Core, items badged by source, click-through deep-links into the right surface. The postMessage contract is already there; the inbox aggregator isn't.

Needs: a Dayforce-side inbox aggregator that consumes Flex Work's workflow events; the events are already firing via Integration 05.

04

Temp-to-perm in 30 seconds.

The architecture document defines Integration 06 — flip workerType from ContingentWorker to Employee, end-date the contingent assignment, open the primary WorkAssignment, drop from the supplier's invoice scope. Make this a single guided panel in the worker detail screen, not an Implementation-Guide procedure. The most emotional moment in the contingent lifecycle deserves a real product surface.

Needs: a guided conversion panel on Worker detail; tenure + qualifications + headcount validation surfaced inline; one approval submit calls the workflow chain. contractor-onboard.jsx is the model.

05

Skills round-trip with Dayforce Talent.

Dayforce Talent maintains a skills cloud per employee. Contingent workers come in with skills from the supplier; we should write them back to the Employee row (filtered for tenure / verification). On the buyer side, the requisition's must-have skills should pull from Talent's ontology so a "Senior Java Developer" means the same thing on a badged JR and a contingent JR.

Needs: bidirectional skills sync; reuse JobAssignment.Qualifications as the carrier. Closes the loop between contingent-as-talent-pipeline and badged hiring.

06

Contingent on the Dayforce org chart.

Today the Dayforce Org Chart shows badged employees. Add contingent workers (visually distinguished — dotted border, supplier badge) so site managers see actual headcount on the ground, not paper headcount. Reuses the WorkAssignment sync; the only new work is the visual treatment on the Dayforce side.

Needs: Dayforce-side org-chart render tweak; supplier-attribution lookup via ContingentEngagement.supplierId.

07

Dayforce Wallet for contingent on-demand pay.

Dayforce Wallet's on-demand-pay product is a category-leading retention tool for badged. Extend it to contingent workers via the supplier (supplier loads earned wages; worker draws via Wallet). Net new revenue for Dayforce, new retention tool for suppliers, real differentiation for the buyer's frontline workforce. This is the kind of moment no standalone VMS can match.

Needs: a Wallet-side contingent contract; Flex Work pushes earned-but-unbilled hours into the Wallet ledger.

08

Dayforce Copilot answers Flex Work questions.

The product already ships ai-chat.jsx. Expose its data brief (workforce / suppliers / invoices / requisitions / timesheets) to Dayforce Copilot via a structured tool call. A Dayforce user asks "Which suppliers are off-rate for Plant #02 this quarter?" and Copilot answers — without switching apps. Distinguishes Dayforce Copilot from every HR-only AI assistant on the market.

Needs: a tool-calling contract between Copilot and Flex Work's data brief; reuse the aiChat assistant's read paths.

The Dayforce-native closing argument

Customers don't buy data-model adoption — they buy moments. The architecture is paid for. The wire is paid for. What we owe the customer next is eight screens they can't get anywhere else: the WFM-to-requisition handoff, the unified inbox, the 30-second temp-to-perm panel, the total-workforce headcount tile, the cross-product Copilot, contingent on the org chart, Wallet for contingent, and skills round-trip with Talent.

Each of those is a Q's worth of work for a small joint team. Ship four of the eight and the Dayforce-native pitch is undeniable — and uncloneable.

05 · Sequencing & bets

Now → Next → Horizon — what gets shipped in what order.

The three rungs are not parallel. Frontline funds the references that buy us the right to be in Vndly evaluations; Helios depth makes Dayforce-native a complete pitch instead of an HCM-only one. The order matters more than the totals.

One closing observation

Read the three sections back to back and a single thread emerges: the platform is more ready than the product. Every gap in this document — every single one — sits on top of primitives we already model. The unglamorous work of the last 18 months (type-blind data, Dayforce-adopted models, feature-flagged migration, sibling objects, the iframe embed contract) is the moat. What it earns us now is the right to ship product surfaces at speed without re-platforming.

The next 12 months are not "build a better VMS." They are "compose the VMS we already have into the moments customers will pay for" — and trust that the architecture is going to hold while we do it.
