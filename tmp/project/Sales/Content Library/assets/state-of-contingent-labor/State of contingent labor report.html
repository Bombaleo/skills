State of contingent labor 2026

The size of the shift to flexible labor, where programs leak money today, and why buyers are consolidating onto a vendor management system. A briefing you can send before the first conversation.

Edition 2026 research brief Coverage United States Reading time 6 minutes All figures sourced and dated below

The headline

Contingent labor is now a structural part of how work gets done — and most of it is happening outside any system of record. The opportunity is not to hire fewer flexible workers. It is to see, govern and pay for the ones you already have.

01 · The market

The shift is structural, not cyclical

The contingent workforce is no longer an edge case in workforce planning. It is a standing category of spend that rivals permanent payroll in many enterprises, and buyer intent points to more of it, not less.

The composition matters as much as the size. Independent contractors alone accounted for $690.6 billion of that B2B spend in 2023, with statement-of-work consultants and platform workers growing in importance — categories that rarely flow through a staffing agency or a single contract. The work is real, ongoing and business-critical. The visibility usually is not.

"84% of labor and 86% of revenue comes from sources other than staffing in the gig economy."
Adam Pode, Senior Director of Research, Staffing Industry Analysts — author, The US Gig Economy 2024

02 · The leak

Where unmanaged programs lose money

When flexible labor is sourced ad hoc — a hiring manager engaging a contractor directly, an invoice approved without a rate check — the spend does not disappear. It simply stops being visible, and visibility is where savings and compliance both come from.

~60%
Spend off the books
Of all contingent and contract labor is unaccounted for in the average company's financial planning and forecasting.
Source: Ardent Partners, via Worksuite
5–16%
Savings lost to maverick spend
Of negotiated savings is lost annually to maverick (off-contract) buying when purchases bypass approved processes.
Source: The Hackett Group
up to 80%
Invoices off-process
Of all invoices can be maverick buying in organizations that lack proper purchasing controls.
Source: CIPS (Chartered Institute of Procurement & Supply)

Beyond hard dollars, unmanaged programs carry worker-classification and co-employment risk. When no system enforces tenure limits, rate cards or onboarding checks, the question of whether a long-tenured contractor is really an employee is left to chance — and the penalties for getting it wrong fall on the buyer, not the supplier. The same gaps make duplicate suppliers, rate creep and invoice errors hard to even detect.

What this means

The first dollar of value in a managed program is almost never a rate cut. It is simply turning invisible spend into visible spend — which is exactly what a vendor management system is for.

03 · The benchmarks

Where the spend sits today

SIA breaks US contingent B2B spend into categories of gig work. The mix shows why a single staffing relationship can never give a buyer the full picture — most of the money flows through channels a staffing agency never touches.

For context on the agency channel specifically, SIA projected the US staffing industry at $184.6 billion in 2024 — a fraction of total contingent spend. The takeaway for a buyer is simple: managing your staffing suppliers well still leaves the majority of your flexible-labor spend ungoverned.

04 · The response

The shift toward consolidated VMS programs

The market's answer to fragmented, invisible spend is consolidation onto a vendor management system — one place to post requisitions, onboard and govern suppliers, approve timesheets, and pay against agreed rates. Adoption is now a mature, multi-billion-dollar category in its own right.

$4.5B
Spent annually on vendor management systems
SIA, VMS Market Developments Report
29%
Of indirect spend is off-contract without a system to enforce it
The Hackett Group

A VMS earns its place by closing the loop the data above describes: it makes off-program spend visible, enforces rate cards and tenure rules automatically, and matches invoices to approved work so errors never reach payment. For organizations already running payroll, HR and time on one platform, the natural next step is a VMS that lives in the same system rather than bolting on a separate tool.
